Empathetic Avatars

As my Masters Thesis project, I designed and developed a computer game called Merlynne to investigate the effect of avatar stereotypes on prosocial behaviour in virtual environments. Merlynne asked players to provide advice to reddit queries within a fantasy game environment. Findings from a mixed-methods study suggested that stereotypically helpful avatars motivated empathetic advice.

OCT 2017 - SEPT 2019

As a game developer and designer, I used the Unity engine (C#) and Visual Studio to create Merlynne and Photoshop CS5 to render the art assets. Google sheets was used to store player data from Unity.

As a researcher, I conducted a mixed-methods study using automatically logged usage data from Unity and semi-structured interviews with players, using SPSS, Excel, and Overleaf to analyze and present results.

Summary

Researchers have explored how online communities can be leveraged for peer support, but general disinterest and a lack of engagement have emerged as substantial barriers to their use in practice. To address this gap, I used the Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM) and games user research, to create a serious game that motivates individuals to become peer supporters using the Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) techniques.

I conducted a mixed-methods, exploratory study to evaluate Merlynne's design and specifically studied the Proteus Effect, hypothesizing that players using a stereotypically helpful avatar would have higher usage rates and a higher change in helping attitudes scores (HAS) than players using a stereotypically unhelpful avatar.

Problem

The Proteus Effect is when an individual is influenced by the stereotypes of their avatar in virtual spaces to act differently in reality. I wished to determine if using avatars with stereotypically helpful appearances can motivate male-identifying individuals in particular to participate in peer to peer support through a game interface.

Findings were to be interpreted as design suggestions to improve online mental health platform engagement, an identified problem in literature and industry.

Approach

Merlynne was created in consultation with health groups, ethics boards, HCI experts, and existing literature in HCI and mental health.

Determining Content

Early discussions with mental health and education groups supported the idea of gamified peer support, with additional interest in the way male-identifying individuals used online peer support services. We had planned to use real-time reddits posts from university subreddits but due to ethics limitations it was important for posts to be de-identified and approved beforehand. We instead used paraphrased reddit posts representative of themes discussed on university subreddits. Participants were convinced of its authenticity in interviews.

Iterative Design

Early proofs of concepts of different games designs were presented for roundtable discussions with HCI experts. Raised issues included problematic narratives and incentives to cheat. As a solution, we consulted literary expertise and involved social elements to dissuade cheating (changing to peer support instead of solidary gameplay).

Prototypes of Merlynne underwent playtests with graduate student colleagues using talk-alouds and interviews. Confusions in flow and causes of breaks in immersion were identified, and changes were applied between each playtest.

For the purpose of my MSc research on the Proteus Effect, two avatar conditions (Monster and Cleric) were created based on literary research from the media arts field with feedback from peers studying language in gaming.

Research

A 2 hr in-lab study was held with 36 male participants recruited from a graduate mailing list and posters from the University of Waterloo campus. Participants completed pre-post surveys containing HAS scales before and after a 30-40 minute play session using a random avatar condition, followed by a semi-structured interview.

Peer support responses collected in-game from participants were assessed for adherence to CBT format by three researchers (with high inter-rater reliability). Data-driven thematic analysis using open and axial coding was used to interpret interviews, supported by inferential statistics with non-parametric tests (Mann Whitney U) of usage data including aformentioned responses coded for CBT format adherence.

Reflection

My thesis was successfully defended, and the Merlynne tool was packaged to be accessible to future researchers looking into peer support through RPG interfaces.

What I learned
Through this project, I adopted a systems-based thinking due to the many moving parts (development, content, research, feedback) within a short time frame. A major personal takeaway was the need to self-organize and learn quickly .

What I would have done differently:
  • Selective sampling of persons knowledgeable of RPG mediums to test the Proteus Effect specifically. The Proteus Effect requires the user to recognize stereotypes associated with avatars. Although tested for, many participants revealed they were not familiar with fantasy rpg archetypes in interviews. This change will reduce the generalizability of study results, yet as our purpose was exploratory study focusing on the presence of the Proteus Effect, selective sampling will provide a more accurate measurement.

  • Modded a pre-existing game to reduce barriers for immersion due to low fidelity of aesthetics and design.

  • Involved the real concerns of individuals, and asked them to validate the effectiveness of the peer support received. Although researchers rated the reponses, we could not measure for true helpfulness given they were not the original posters.

  • Recorded gameplay footage either through video or keylogging to understand the participants' gameplay choices. Where the player was clicking, travelling with their avatar, and how they structured their responses was not documented in our study, but the qualitative data could hold insight into the usage data. For example, if a player spent longer than usual on a level, were they exploring the map, rethinking their responses, or just idle? Eye-tracking would also show player behaviour, especially in measuring the amount of attention they gave to the avatar but that would have higher set-up costs and risk inconsistencies.

Specific to technical development, there were many Unity techniques I later learned which would have benefited Merlynne such as linking all text objects to a database (e.g. Firebase, or a spreadsheet) for easier content management, using tags to classify NPCs, and using Scriptable Objects to manage the status of interactive objects.

I would have also stored the incoming data natively to an excel spreadsheet to reduce the risk of losing Google conenctivity or the status of my account if it were to be used by future researchers.

Next Steps and Questions

The following questions were identified:

  • How familiar should a person be with an archetype for the Proteus Effect to occur? If the Proteus Effect occurred and suggestions were made to designers about which types of avatars to use, recent and classic media trends would need to be considered. If a current trend was to rewrite a traditional archetype (ie. a helpful monster or a damaging cleric), how long would this trend need to exist for to be relevant to the Proteus Effect? Alternatively, if designers were to build a character behind an avatar, how well would the player need to be familiar with the character?

  • What is appropriate feedback for peer support? Participants anticipated more motivation to help if their responses were validated to be helpful, either in-game or otherwise. Designing appropriate feedback that encourages peer participation without encouraging harmful participation would be an area of future research

  • Are there gender differences? Given our participant pool of male-identifying individuals, other genders may yield different results. The relationship between genders and gendered avatars is of interest.
In final reflection, I believe the philosophical question of whether peer support should be encouraged with gamification needs to be revisited, as in-game incentives can lessens the reflection of impact of support. Mental health expert involvement is suggested.

First aid kit
for mental health

I took an idea from a pitch competition and turned it into a sustainable venture with international sales with the goal of delivering stress management tools to populations facing high-stress.

2016 - now

PASS inc. is a self-sutaining social venture:

My research on the PASS kit involved design based on literature on stress management, and running a pre-post comparative study with over 400 participants between two residences at the University of Waterloo in 2016. Results suggested students with the PASS kit felt more confident in dealing with daily stress.

As a product designer, I balanced costs with customer needs to design, resource, and develop products which have been sold worldwide, leading to a profitable business.

*As the company founder, I took on many, many other roles including accounting, sales, and team management.

Summary

The Panic, Anxiety, and Stress Support (PASS) kit was born out of my undergraduate thesis research exploring methods to bring stress management symptoms to students during moments of high stress. Each kit includes a squeezable stress star, ear plugs, a sleeping mask, a pack of gum and a deck of 25 flash cards with steps a person can take when they are experiencing anxiety or stress symptoms. It is a sustainable social venture.

Research & Design

The PASS kit was designed through initial round-table disccusions with students, followed by a comparative study between two residences on the Unversity of Waterloo campus with just under 400 participants.

The PASS kit contains:

  • Flashcards based on CBT encouraging users to re-think their stress symptoms while providing straightforward actions for stress management
  • Ear Plugs to block out environmental stressors of noise
  • Sleeping Mask to block out environmental stressors of light
  • Chewing Gum for grounding exercises and exercisng jaw muscles for bette bloodflow
  • Foam Star to tense and release muscles to assist in the progressive muscle relaxation technique

Further iterations were based on discussions with customers who shared their needs. Through discussion with customer we

learned:

  • Informative packaging is needed to instruct end-users on the purpose of the kit.

  • Rectangular containers were optimal for shipping.

  • Clear packaging opens up more opportunities for custom branding to be seen through inserts.

Variations over the years are documented below.

Impact

The PASS inc. has since:

  • Distributed 50 thousand products worldwide, bringing stress management information to schools, workplaces, and communities

  • Created jobs for students and adults from disadvantaged populations seasonally

  • Inspired others through invited talks and workshops

Interactive display

I contributed to the design and created the art assets of Illuminate, an interactive museum display which emphasized hope in a future with climate change.

OCT - DEC, 2019

My consultation work on Illuminate involved coordinating complex interdisciplinary research in unfamiliar areas of study, using game development processes in an academic space, and translating scientific concepts in meaningful ways to a generalist audience.

As lead artist, I worked concurrently with the developer and created art assets in Adobe Photoshop for Unity. I also performed user-testing, and designed an art style (ie. line weight, colour) friendly to non-artists tasked with scaling the Illuminate project in the future.

Urban context Coastal context Rural context

Summary

The MUSEUM in Kitchener, Ontario held the ALARM exhibition to press the urgency of action for climate change. In cooperation with the The Interdisciplinary Centre on Climate Change (IC3), the Games Institute contracted myself and others to develop an interactive public display to demonstrate current and future steps which can be done to mitigate effects of climate change to daily life. Titled Illuminate, the display used a visual novel game interface to feature three Canadian landscapes (Coastal, Urban, and Rural) which would change depending on adaptations (ie. solar panels, building regulations) chosen by museum guests in a narrative. Illuminate was to be on display in the MUSEUM in early 2020.

Problem

IC3 recognized the need to educate families with young children on steps already being done to mitigate effects of climate change on daily life to convey hope in their future.

Approach

A core production team comprised of a writer, developer, and artist (myself) was tasked with creating an installation encouraging hope in The MUSEUM's ALARM exhibit, with administrative staff support managing communications with IC3 and The MUSEUM. The project kicked off in November 2019 with January 2020 in collaboration with climate scientists of IC3. Discussions lead to acknowledgment of the short runway for the project from all parties and decided on a module-based game for Illuminate. Three playable modules were to be completed by January 2020, with opportunities for more modules to be added in early February 2020 if needed. Two weeks of testing time was scheduled into the plan.

Workflow Management

Recurring meetings took place within the production team (writer, developer, artist), and separately within research team (writer, administrative staff, IC3) to support concurrent workflow wwith competing schedules. Communications were exchanged internally on Slack and externally via email and a shared OneDrive. The management system was consistently used and effectively led to a scalable prototype developed by the time the writing was finalized.

Considering Legacy

The developer and I (artist) were not scheduled to continue our contracts, thus if new modules were to be added post-January, both the game environment and the art assets must be easily learnable by new members. In the case no new art talent was available, I purposefully used a low-barrier art style. Barriers to artwork were seen as poor art fundamentals such as (1) line control; (2) colour theory; and (3) drawing from memory. Thus, the art style I chose used feathered brushes which did not require precise line weight control, a colour palette was pre-chosen for future art, and realistic proportions were used for future artwork to be produced by tracing photos.

Design

Iluminate's design needed to attract and engage museum guests in a large public space to on handheld tablet. Guest attention was a rare commodity among other attractive displays and their time may be limited. Thus,

  • the game loop must be quick for guests to quickly take away a learnable experience
  • the installation as a whole must be attractive to convince guests to approach the display

MUSEUM space Game loops were kept short (30 secs to 2 min play), asking players only three questions each landscape scenario, with 3-4 options per question to ensure replayability. A second monitor with a trailer video was also set up to display potential gameplay to passersby to garner interest in interacting.

Testing

As a development team, we wished to deliver a product by January with understandable flows accurate to the latest climate science and fit The MUSEUM's branding.

Expert Review

A finished prototype of one landscape (coastal) was presented as a demo to IC3, with the narrative and artwork of remaining landscapes provided as text and image files. Through email discussions and a working shared spreadsheets file, IC3 provided feedback in writing with relevant sources. Narrative and artwork was updated with their insight.

Students were asked to play through scenarios and voice aloud their thoughts and concerns during the playthroughs. Inconsistencies between narrative and art were brought up, as well as minor confusions in UI (e.g. buttons were not obviously leading to intended screens). Changes were applied quickly from testing and tested again internally before launching.

Impact

Illuminate was ready for installation for the MUSEUM in January 2020 in the MUSEUM, successfully:

  • Educating potential MUSEUM guests on possible adaptations to climate change

  • Providing a scalable model for an interactive public display if IC3 and the Games Institute wishes to expand on the project to incorporate other landscapes such as artics, suburbs, or mountains

  • Creating an artifact for research for the Games Institute for future research opportunities and display at open house events

Reflection

The project was successful in process and delivery with strengths in teamwork and transparency across parties during the development process despite competing priorities during the short runway.

What I learned
I learned to ask the right questions from subject matter experts to make sure artwork was representative of their messaging, and organizing art assets in ways which would save the developer time.

What I would have done differently

I prioritized art assets for the Illuminate interface, but in retrospect the art should have considered the installation concept as a whole from print advertisements to video trailers. The colour schemes and iconography chosen for the gameplay were lighter in colour and storybook-like to appeal to both children (storybook readers) and adults (storybook buyers). Although effective for the visual-novel game interface, the style may not be as attractive to the passerby in a busy public space, especially when compared to previous displays by the Games Institute like Quantum Cats, which has graphics easily translatable to its promotional materials.

In the future I would also use vectors for images for better scaling across different screen sizes and I would be interested in exploring the .psb file setting instead of saving each layer (through batch processing script) to be imported into Unity to enable animations.

Supporting Lean Startup Development

We hosted a post-hackathon event inviting seniors with lived experiences to help hackathon winners refine their health 'hacks' for commercialization. Follow-up interviews revealed the strengths of using Learning Circles, an indiginous model of knowledge sharing, in the software development life cycle (SDLC).

APR - DEC, 2019

As an event planner, I communicated with stakeholders to ensure their attendance, coordinated day-of logistics, and created Qualtrics surveys to manage voting and feedback.

As a researcher, I performed thematic analysis on interviews with NVivo to interpret and present findings into a case study to publish at an ACM conference, CHI 2020.

Summary

In the summer of 2019, two concurrent Hacking Health (HH) hackathons were held in Kitchener and in Ottawa with the annual theme of social isolation in seniors. I provided mentorship at the Kitchener event and was an organizer of the post-hackathon co-creation event at the True North 2019 conference. The post-hackathon event invited stakeholders including seniors with lived experiences to offer hackers feedback through Learning Circles -a facilitated discussion prioritizing the equality of opinions. To assess the merits of the post-hackathon event, I performed thematic analysis on interviews with hackers after the event and revealed addressable barriers and strengths to using Learning Circles in the SDLC.

Problem

Hackathons provide rapid, hands-on opportunities to explore innovative solutions to problems, but provide little support to teams in moving those solutions into practice. This is observed of health hacks in particular due to healthcare's high barrier to entry.

Approach

We explore the use of post-hackathon Learning Circles to connect hackathon teams with key stakeholders, to reflect on prototypes and consider business models. Learning Circles were used due to their documented success in student discussions and the Agile method of software development to encourage reflection and sharing among interdisciplinary teams.

Learning Circles
Mentors made up of health professionals (e.g. physicians, LHIN representatives), technical experts (e.g. software developers, UX designers), business professionals, academics, and seniors from the community formed Learning Circles with hackers around large tables. The circle formation was designed to recognize the equity of voices within interdisciplinary groups, and facilitators emphasized this approach at the beginning of each session. Each session started off with a brief introduction by each member and followed with unmoderated discussion usually prompted by questions by hackers.

Interviews
We conducted a qualitative field study with 4 post-hackathon teams, interviewing 13 hackers over Zoom within two weeks of the post-hackathon event. First interviews were attended by 2-3 researchers with additional researchers performing member checks with the interviewer to ensure the validity of participant responses.

NVivo was used for thematic analysis where three researchers independently coded interviews while reflexively journaling. Codes were compared and themes were identified with group discussion.

Results

Our results show that Learning Circles are an effective way to involve stakeholders early in the development process, and to develop a deeper understanding of users, markets, and technology. Themes were:

  • Building a shared understanding. Learning Circles bridge interdisciplinary teams engaged in different phases of the development life cycle.
  • Stakeholder culture clash. Seniors were less encouraging of entrepreneurship and more focused on whether the solutions solved problems they understood. Making seniors aware of the 'appreciative rather than critical' expectations of feedback in hackathon culture may improve perceived value of their input.
  • Mentor preferences. Although participant equity was emphasized in Learning Circles, teams prioritized certain mentors' time over others. Inviting mentors with experiences specific to particular teams (e.g. experiences with physiotherapy if a team is working on a project related to injury recovery) may be advised in the future without sacrificing interdisciplinarity.

Impact

Through the post-hackathon project I have:

  • Advanced business development by connecting early stage startups to stakeholders in public and private sectors.
  • Builded a community of interdisciplinary mentors interested in supporting local health technology development for older adults.
  • Extended hackathon research at an international ACM conference, with interest of follow-up by international researchers.

Post-hackathon Learning Cirles pose as a method to accelerate startup developement and has attracted interest from other researchers in the hackathon space for data comparison.

Reflection

Post-hackathon Learning Circles may be effective in ideation and product/market validation stages of the startup development from themes identified, but follow-up is needed to understand their direct impact on participating hackers. Future research might explore an extended hackathon format and use of Learning Circles to reduce development time, startup acceleration, and contribution to innovation ecosystem capacity.

What I learned
Through this project I practiced effective teamwork and document management in a short timeline, and was able to keep flexible hours concurrently with my schoolwork at the time without impacting either.
What I would have done differently

When recruiting mentors for the post-hackathon event, I managed three email inboxes, with unique email templates and used a shared Google Spreadsheet with multiple parties. One surprising lesson I learned is that many email inboxes automatically flag emails with the word 'hack' in it, especially academic and government domains. It would have been wise to use an address without the word 'hackathon.' Other than this small lesson, given the resource and time constraints of this project, I believe all decisions were well made.

SmartSurveys

I implemented SmartSurveys, a research tool prompting consumers to provide service feedback via smartphones based on geographic location ('geolocation'). The study goal was to identify addressable barriers that impact the adoption of smartphone technology to gather patient experience data in health care spaces.

APR 2017 - DEC 2018

As a project leader, I kept meeting agendas, coordinated undergradaute research assistants, and kept all parties updated on all components of a project through shared tracking documents.

As a researcher, I wrote provincial grants (OCE) and ethics applications to two university boards (Wilfrid Laurier University (WLU), University of Waterloo (UW)), while creating the SmartSurveys on MetricWire and its onboarding materials with their app developers.

Summary

Healthcare providers (ie. pharmacists, family physicians, chiropractors) request patient experience feedback to improve their workflow and care, but risk patients's poor memory with delayed phonecalls and paper surveys are inconvenient to administer in high traffic areas. Smartsurveys presents as a solution by prompting patients with electronic surveys when they enter a predetermined ('geofenced') location such as a doctor's office. As a researcher, I organized a field study in a team of 4 for 200 participants within the Kitchener-Waterloo community, while co-supervised by Dr. Josephine McMurray of WLU and Dr. James Wallace of UW. The study design was presented at the 2018 ACM International Conference on Interactive Surfaces and Spaces.

Problem

Providers need affordable and effective ways to collect feedback, and smartphone applications present as suitable solutions. However, previous research shows that patients are hesitant to provide smartphone-based feedback in a care setting due to perceived risks and apparent futility of expecting change as a result.

Approach

I extended the work of a previous graduate student who observed that patients had privacy concerns about sharing health information over personal smartphones. The second phase of the project aimed to understand:

  • Context Sensitivity and whether individuals would actually report less information to health providers.
  • Reasons for declining to give service feedback to providers of any type
A field study was designed to measure individuals' willingness to provide feedback to health providers compared to non-health service providers (ie. banks, theatres, car maintenance shops) using the MetricWire app to host the study tool.

MetricWire is a data collection tool developed in Kitchener-Waterloo by the startup MetricWire Inc., who were also collaborators on the NSERC-Engage grant funding SmartSurveys. In regular meetings with the MetricWire team, I came to discover the limitations and opportunities with their tool concurrently with their startup development.

Research

I led the implementation of the SmartSurveys tool, which included its onboarding materials, development, and testing.

Study Design and Materials

Participants were recruited online and directed to enroll in the SmartSurveys study on the MetricWire app through a one-page website. The website linked to consent letters and installation guides hosted on Google Drive. When SmartSurveys was installed, participants would complete an intro survey to complete enrollment, then be given surveys through MetricWire every time they dwelled in a geofenced location.

Automatically measured variables included situated usage data such as time spent in a geographic area, time between entering a location and submitting feedback, and between the time of exit and feedback submission. Overall activity data like the number of submissions, and the number of words per submission was also tracked to be compared between locations.

Tool Development

I trained two undergraduate research assistants to collect the geographical coordinates and radial size of service providers in Kitchener-Waterloo based on Google map data.

Four lists of geofences were created, containing 100-150 locations on each list.

  • On Campus* - Health Provider
  • Off Campus** - Health Provider
  • On Campus - Non-health Provider
  • Off Campus - Non-health Provider

* On the WLU or UW campus

** In the Kitchener-Waterloo region

Tool Testing

After the locations were programmed into the SmartSurveys tool, we tested the sensitivity of the MetricWire app to geofences on foot using different smartphones and operating systems (iPhone, Android).

There were overlapping geofences due to multi-service buildings with multiple floors with different services. It was difficult to differentiate services leading to the over-triggering of surveys. As a solution, an extra question was asked in multi-service buildings asking participants to indicate where they were in a form field.

Some geofences also did not fire when entered, as Android phones were not registering the geofence as quickly to save battery life. This posed as a study problem as iPhone and Android users would have inconsistent participant experiences. As a short-term fix, I changed the trigger from 'dwelling in an area' to 'exiting an area' to at least ensure all participants received notifications after service provision instead of some during and some after.

Results

Project is currently on hold.

Impact

My involvement with SmartSurveys contributed to

  • Ethics approval for a cross-campus field study
  • A GPS-triggered survey tool for collecting consumer feedback for service providers
  • Presentation on research methods at an international ACM conference
Publications

Chan, T., McMurray, J., Wallace, J. R., & Sidahmed, A.. (2018). SmartSurveys: Does context influence whether well share healthcare experience data with our smartphone? . In ACM Conference on Interactive Surfaces and Spaces 2018. Association for Computing Machinery. Retrieved from https://dl.acm.org/citation.cfm?id=3279912

Reflection

Technological barriers to implementation remained after my involvement, but a good foundation was set for the project to continue once problems were fixed. Strengths were in project organization leading to quick and effective meetings given the competing priorities of all parties.

What I learned
Through this project I learned a lot about knowledge translation and project management within a interdiscinplinary team with remote team members.

What I would have done differently

One major barrier to implementation was the inconsistency of geofences firing for different smartphone users. Despite the investment in tool setup, I would have explored other ways to measure contextual sensitivity.

The study could have sent participants surveys to complete through MetricWire according to a predetermined time schedule. Participants would report where they were and offer feedback if willing. Although less relevant data points may be collected (e.g. if the participant was at home most of the survey times), it would have been independent of GPS error.

Game Consultation

LIFEHACK was created by youth at a hackathon in Ulaanbataar to teach Mongolian students about the effects of daily choices based on the creators' own experiences. I proposed design and development methods to reduce their future costs for commercialization.

SEPT - NOV , 2018

As a consultant, I scheduled meetings over Google hangouts, and applied my understanding of social determination theory (SDT) and the agile software development to the context of serious games.

As a researcher, I documented methods and findings for presentation at a local conference, and identified lessons learned which can be applied to future global health projects.

Summary

In communications with Unicef Mongolia and Dr. Plinio Morita of the Ubiquitous Computing Lab at the University of Waterloo, my colleague Marcela Costa and I advised the development of LIFEHACK, an educational simulation game made by youth in Ulaanbataar around sexual health, drug use, and other topics. The result was an example of global collaboration using local knowledge of the startups and the software development life cycle (SDLC) in Kitchener-Waterloo, and local knowledge of youth experiences in Ulaanbataar to co-create health products. The project was presented at a Toronto conference on global health.

Problem

An initial meeting over Skype with the LIFEHACK team was facilitated by a translator (Russian to English, English to Russian) from Unicef Mongolia. Mock-ups and pitch decks previously provided were reviewed and the following problems were shared:

  • Difficulty sustaining player engagement

  • Lack of development talent

It became known in future meetings that a prototype needed to be developed for LIFEHACK to renew funding from Unicef Mongolia, thus the short runway for development was acknowledged.

Approach

The original approach proposed by LIFEHACK was to create 6 personas the player can play as through 6 different lifestyles, through 12 different topics. Each persona and topic combination would feature unique narrative, art, and minigames (ie. a basketball hoop shooting game for the fitness-related topic) to add interest to the narrative gameplay. Although comprehensive, the proposed content and development heavy approach was misaligned with the LIFEHACK team's timeline.

In review of the LIFEHACK's vision, resources, and talent, the teams' strengths laid in their lived experiences as youth growing up in Mongolia, connections to experts in Unicef Mongolia, and artistic talent (graphic design, photography, writing).

Through two more video calls, we further understood the vision of LIFEHACK to bring the game into middle school classrooms, and shared our intentions to use concepts from Self-Determination Theory (SDT) and iterative testing in the SDLC to help them develop their project.

All opinions were presented as suggestions in the interest of faster development, and it was made clear that they were to prioritize their vision if they saw conflict in suggestions.

Proposal

The following suggestions were presented to the LIFEHACK team prioritizing prototype development and scaling:

  • Prototype. Focus on creating one storyline (one persona, one topic) that has a clear flow and thoughtful content. A minimally viable prototype (MVP) allows for early testing to identify issues and inconsistencies before investing in scaling more content. The perfected flow (including research processes and internal team management systems) can then be replicated for more storylines to reduce technical debt. Developers can be later hired to create high-fidelity products from a tested prototype.

  • Focus on narrative. Given the limited programming talent on the Mongolian team, it was advised that minigames would be difficult to create in short time. The assumption that narrative alone cannot be engaging should be tested, as narrative content can be created quickly with art talent and little programming.

  • Introduce multiplayer. Providing social relatedness, a psychological need according to SDT, could contrsibute to intrinsic motivation to continue play. If designed for classroom play, social elements could encourage discussion to explore imapcts of life choices beyond the game.

Impact

In consideration of proposed changes, LIFEHACK chose to focus on one topic (drug use), and consider other elements in future designs.

This consultation effort was presented as a poster as an example of global collaboration health research and game development.

Publications

Chan, T., Costa, M., Somani, N., Morita, P., & Wallace, J. R.. (2018). LIFEHACK - A Serious Game and Simulation by Mongolian Youth to Educate and Motivate Students to make Healthy Choices in Mongolia. Global Health Students and Young Professionals Summit, 2018.

Reflection

The consultation project demonstrated how local development knowledge from the Kitchener-Waterloo region could be applied to the development of products in international markets. It was an honour being a part of LIFEHACK's development and I believe it will have a positive impact on Mongolian classrooms when successful.

What I learned
Through this project I was able to learn about needs for software development in a global health context such as funding cycles and mobilizing talent effectively. As I was an outside consultant, I practiced appreciative inquiry and refrained from paternalistic suggestions to respect the Mongolian team's vision.' I also deliberately made conversation points concise for effective translation over video calls.

What I would have done differently

It was assumed that the availability of developers and classrooms in Mongolia were similar to the Canadian environment. If time and opportunity had allowed it, it would have been worthwhile understanding the startup and game development ecosystem (e.g. talent costs) in Ulaanbaatar outside of LIFEHACK's experiences to provide more insightful advice.

Problem & Approach

In a series of semi-structured interviews with mentors with lived experiences with MS present at H4H, we identified fatigue as a consistent worry of MS patients. Fatigue was limiting their abilities and motivation to participate in daily activities. Clinicians and neurodegenerative disease experts were subsequently consulted on potential solutions to fatigue, and regular exercise was cited as a benefit. However, there was difficulty motivating continuous exercising habits.

We returned to literature research to find MS had its highest prevalence in women, with the common age of onset between 25-35 years. For the duration of the hackathon we chose to focus on designing a product which can motivate young women recently diagnosed with MS to manage their fatigue.

Personas
We first created a persona of a young woman with MS who was:
  • A digital native
  • spirational in school and career goals
  • A kitchener-waterloo resident

Her goals:

  • Actively managing her MS symptoms
  • Maintain energy levels for daily tasks

Her frustrations:

  • MS resources were aimed at older adults
  • Being seen differently with MS
  • Taking time out of her schedule for MS-related check-ups in Toronto

In considering the persona, we decided the app needed to:

  • Not be branded in a way which implies treatment or pathology
  • Have an appearance competitive to current trendy fitness products (ie. fitbit)
  • Motivate returning use without sense of punishment
  • Functions are not limited by time or geography

Design

A mobile app 'Roze' was designed as our hack submission. Roze would deliver videos of exercises appropriate for persons with MS by MS experts, and the user could indicate when they have completed an exercise. Progress was visualized as a growing plant to represent the continued growth of the MS journey, and new variations of leaves, flowers, or other plants would be available with constant logging of exercise. Badges and awards would become available for challenges such as trying a new exercise, or continually exercising for a set number of days. It is assumed the woman with MS is already interested in exercise for fatigue management upon app usage.

Summary

The 5in5 Pharmacy project was an experimental intiative in 2014 by Dr. Kelly Grindrod from the UW School of Pharmacy. The goal was to update practicing pharmacists' knowledge of the expanding scope of practice in Alberta and Ontario through short five-minute online modules. As a UX Researcher on this project, I developed a MVP to use in interviews with several community pharmacies at their place of work to understand their needs for extended education. Problem

In 2014, Ontario and Alberta pharmacists had an expanded their scopes of practice to be authorized to perform adaptations, renewals, and injections. However, the University of Waterloo's School of Pharmacy recognized hesitations from practicing community pharmacists to perform new duties due to perceived lack of training. Although workshops and lunch and learns for additional training were available to pharmacists, there was poor information retaining a period after the event, and pharmacists reported a lack of free time to commit to sessions (regardless of online or in person).

Approach

Prior to my involvement, a small grant was received to explore the design and development of a short, modular, online continuing education course for pharmacists. The modular format allowed flexible completion throughout the day with repetitive and continuously updating content over time. The tool was named '5in5' as every course had 5 modules with 5 questions which could be completed in 5 minutes.

There was interest in exploring gamification to sustain the motivation of the pharmacist to use 5in5, but further research was required to understand the needs and wants of practicing pharmacists.

Wireframes created by Brad Jennings, a predecessor in my role.

Gameflow:
  1. Registration, showing type of pharmacist, location, education, years in practice
  2. Dashboard of all available and upcoming modules and courses
  3. Quizzes with 5 questions, which pharmacist will complete one by one about their own practice
  4. Score out of 5 is visible and resources on that course is unlocke after all five questions are completed
  5. Return to dashboard with options to re-do module, other modules, or view unlocked resources

Research

I developed an MVP on articulate storyline through iterative design and visited pharmacist to understand their experience with the tool. The goal was to identify gamification elements appropriate to motivating meaningful engagement and reflection for behaviour change in pharmacists.

Contextual Inquiry
Through talk-alouds, video-recorded gameplay, and semi-structured interviews with community pharmacists in their workplaces, we observed the following:
  • Practicing pharmacists were aware of the expanding scope but needed an assurance of their competency.
  • Pharmacists were interested in the actions of peers as well as what was allowed and encouraged by the OCP.
  • Pharamcists requested 5in5 content for offline reference. For example, print-outs due to internet and devices not always being available.

In response to these observations, the following additions were added to the 5in5 design:

  • Wording. We wanted to capture the right answers to persuade change in pharmacy practice. We separated the simulated case scenario and the pharamcist's own behaviour to encourage reflection

    'Would Susan the competent pharmacist renew the blood pressure drug?'

    'Would you?'

    The pharmacist was encourage to question the conflict between their knowledge and actions.

  • Avatars. Each case scenario had a unique avatar to represent a fictional pharmacist to contrast against the customized avatar of the user pharmacist.
  • Score breakdown. Every question would display a percentage of how many other users answered it correctly versus incorrectly imemdiately after completion.
  • Printable posters. The resources were now printable in infogrpahic poster formats to be displayed in offices.

  1. Registration, showing type of pharmacist, location, education, years in practice

  3. Dashboard of all available and upcoming modules and courses
  4. *Case Scenarios with 5 simulations about a fictional 'competent' pharmacist, which pharmacist will complete one by one
  5. *Reflection Questions asking if the pharmacist would practice as they had answered for the fictional pharmacist
  6. *Question score accompanied by the percentage of others who also scored incorrectly
  7. Score out of 5 is visible and resources on that course is unlocke after all five questions are completed
  8. Return to dashboard with options to re-do module, other modules, or view unlocked resources
  9. *Feedback option for pharmacists to sugegst new courses for 5in5

Summary

With my friends and colleagues from the University of Waterloo's Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) research group, we participated in the 2019 Hack 4 Good hacakthon hosted by GeekSpeak commerce in Whitby. The hackathon theme was 'AI' and we developed a mobile app to assist therapy appointment for persons with bi-polar disorder.

Team
Tina Chan (myself): Adoption and Implementation

Marvin Pafla: Backend Development

Kenny Fung: Frontend Development

Problem

A counselling appointment only captures a snapshot client's state, and recounts risk poor memory. For a client with bi-polar disorder, ups and downs of moods may be of interest to trck. However, self-report or scale-based mood surveys have drawbacks as the client can become accustomed to the questions periodically.

Approach

Journalling has been documented in literature to be an effective way of stress-management though reflection and is often prescribed by psychotherapists. We were also inspired by mood-tracking journals due to their simple visualizations, and wished to leverage AI (theme of the hackathon) to automatically determine mood based on journal entry text.

Example of a mood tracking bullet journal.

Design

A recurrent neural net determined sentiment with movie review data sets used for training. (60% accuracy during the hack)

The app's user flow as well as how Journaly would fit into exsiting workflows of therapists and their clients was considered using a systems approach.

App Design
It was important that the app did not appear paternalistic and recognize that consistent journalling or consistent positive moods was what to strive for. Journaly is a patient shared decision making tool, helping patients under their weekly patterns and verbalize to therapists their experiences.

UI design demonstrating user flow created by my team member Andrew Cen who was leading UX design.

Upon login the user would:

  1. View progress, showing how many entries were logged in a week
  2. Log a journal entry or a task. Self-reporting a mood is an option, but sentiment anaysis automaticlally calculates mood as well.
  3. View moods over a calendar which would be colour-coded with a mood score. A blue would represent negative mood, a green would represent a positive mood.

Use in appointment
It was very important for the app to be assistive in an appointment and remained flexible to a therapist's individual methods.

During the appointment:

  1. Patient reviews mood calendar before appointment, refreshing memory of the week.
  2. Shares mood calendar with therapist when appropriate for therapist to get an executive summary of mood changes.
  3. Open journal entry of interest by expanding dates on the coloured calendar if appropriate

Implementation and Adoption
NSERC-Engage grants can fund co-operation with academic research with practicing counselors to improve usability. Academic research could also open collaborations in industry. Counselors involved in studies could become champions for the tool, and government grants such as Ontario Centre of Excellence grants and Federal Government funding for Mental Health and Addictions can fund further development costs if studies validate the concept.

Impact

The development of this hack
  • Received $5000 in prize money.
  • Inspired the 2020 Hack 4 Good Hackathon theme to be 'Biohacking' by drawing attention to the importance of health innovation
  • Created an artifact with future research potential.

Reflection

It is recognized that outside of a hackathon the many assumptions we made in Journaly would need to be validated. The assumption that sentiment scores translate to mood is the core one. Furthermore, as with all assistive technology, we need to confirm that the app does save resources (time) rather than instead require the therapist to conduct another timely task of validating Journaly's report with the patient. Journaly is a successful hack evidenced by the win, and has the potential to become a research artifact or tool for an interested researcher.

What is next?
I personally am not confident in Journaly's commercialization potential due to the saturation of mental health apps in the market, but I believe its visualization and AI-driven components should be considered by existing products. I am also very proud of my team members and teamwork through quick effective decision-making over the 36 hour event. (We also all took care make sure everyone got 8hrs of sleep!)

What I would have done differently

If given more time our team would have looked for a more appropriate dataset than movie reviews to train the AI. It would have also been aspirational to incorporate computer vision to convert diaries on paper to text for analysis. A self-report override for mood logging would also be helpful to improve accuracy.

By clicking or tapping the goose, you accumulate points to unlock wearable clothing.
As a game designer I designed the game flow with my partner and created all art assets within 36 hours.

As a game developer I published Mr. Goose's Dressup on the Play Store with Google Play Console after modifying it to an appropriate .apk file in Unity.

Summary

With my partner in the developer role, Mr. Goose's Dressup was created at a 36 hour game jam. It was both our first time creating a mobile app on Unity. Given the short time we wanted to use a simple game loop (cick for clothes) which could be incrementally scalable (more clothes). Mr. Goose's Dressup was uploaded onto the Google Play store and attracted interest from the University of Waterloo student community.

Gameplay

  1. Player taps/clicks the goose to 'honk'
  2. The numbers at the top of the screen increases by one with each honk
  3. When the number matches or exceeds a number in the grid a the bottom of the screen, a new clothing item is unlocked.
  4. Player taps the item and it appears on the goose
The game ends at 999 honks.

Impact

Mr. Goose Dressup has since:

  • Received 100+ downloads from Google Play

  • Been displayed for students and visitors at GI game jam showcases

  • Inspired others to express interest in making mobile games at the GI game jams.

*Bonus: Goose-themed games were jokingly discouraged for the following GI game jam by organizers because of how 'annoying' Mr. Goose's Dressup was.

Reflection

I had a lot of fun making this game and I was very happy that others enjoyed it as well.

What I learned
I learned about publishing games on the Google Play Console, especially app signing by Google Play; Keys, certificates, and keystores.

What I would have done differently
As we did not plan to scale Mr. Goose's Dressup, a proper content management system was not set up. All assets were hard-coded into Unity scripts, resulting in a rather large file size. If we were to reapproach the project, I would link the game to a database (e.g. Google Sheets) which stored all image files for easier updates (new items) in the future.

Tap to smash!

A two-player planet battle game made for one screen.

JAN 2019

Seen Above: World Smash was originally made for phone screens, but it is also playable on large-screen displays.

As an artist I created all game art including UI elements in World Smash, and configured the files to be appropriate for browser (itch.io) and Android (Google Play).

Summary

The GI Game Jam theme for the term was 'worlds' and my team and I developed an Android game using Unity with the idea of a multiplayer game with one screen. The learning goal for me was to improve my drawing skills over a quick period of time.

Team:

Tina (myself): Art

David Dufour, Phil Downey: Development

Gameplay

  1. Player 1 would pick a planet.
  2. Player 1 would choose two items to equip their planet with. All items have certain stat bonusses.
  3. Player 1 hands the phone to Player 2.
  4. Player 2 would pick a planet.
  5. Player 2 would choose two items to equip their planet with. All items have certain stat bonusses.
  6. Player 2 places phone the table and a countdown starts with instructions to tap the screen when countdown finishes.
  7. The player who taps at a time closest to when the countdown ends wins and the other player's planet explodes. Items chosen bonuses factor into the final score.
  8. Item stats would be revealed over time with a certain number of battles.
The game would reset at the end of every battle.

Impact

World Smash has since:

  • Received downloads in 3 continents on Google Play

  • Been displayed for students and visitors at GI game jam showcases

*Bonus: The game was tested for fun on large screen displays and was recognized as a potential study tool.

Reflection

This was the second mobile game app I created with a team at a game jam, and I was able to apply previous learnings into the project. Content management was in lists which made updating content simple throughout the jam.

What I learned
The file was created in two different versions of Unity (2017 and 2019) and it proved more difficult to convert into an .apk file appropriate for Google Play. There were inconsistences in display and minor differences in code. In re-writing and re-designing sections within the Unity environment, I improved my understanding of Unity.

What I would have done differently
The planets were of different sizes which meant somee items drawn using a larger planet as base model (Jupiter) looked off on a smaller planet (Mercury). If re-approaching the project, I would have made sure all items were appropriate for all planet sizes by design (e.g. floating), or automatically scaled to the size of the planet chosen. If we were given more time, I would have also liked to add more sound effects to the game to give players feedback on their interactions.

Un-skelly the skellies!

A side-scrolling beat em up on browser created in a team for the Global Game Jam 2020. Currently hosted on GitHub.

JAN 2020

As an artist I created game spritesheets and animations.

Summary

The Global Game Jam theme for the 2020 year was 'Repair' and my team and I developed an browser-based game where, as a skeleton doctor, you repair other skeletons by providing them with organs. It was a novel spin on beat-em-ups where you 'beat them up' and you instead heal the injured comically. Javescript and Adobe Photoshop was used.

Team:

Tina (myself): Art

David Dufour, Adam Shepley: Development

Animations

Sprites were created in Adobe Photoshop. The attacks of the skeleton doctor had to align with the head, chest, and abdomen of the skeletons. Seperate spritesheets were made when the skeleton received organs.

Gameplay

  1. Left and Right Arrows to walk back and forth.
  2. When in close proximity with a skeleton, use A, S, or D keys to insert an organ:
  3. A: Insert Lungs
  4. S: Insert Eyes
  5. D: Insert Guts
    6. The organs must be inserted in the ASD order. Some skeletons already have lungs or eyes.
  6. Repair 15 skeletons can without incurring too much damage to win.

Impact

Dr. Skelly has since:

  • Been displayed for students and visitors at GI game jam showcases

Reflection

This was my first global game jam, and it was very exciting to be working alongside game makers all across the world in one weekend. I was very happy in our browser-based game since it could be easily enjoyed by anyone when given the link.

What I learned
I gained a lot of experience creating frame-based animations and spritesheets. When planning for animations to interact with eachother, it was important for me to have guidelines to make sure artwork lined up and made sense to the player.

What I would have done differently
I would have used a more consistent naming convention for all the animation frames. I had difficuty predicting the number of frames and sprite sets as development moved on. Next time I would use a number system that is both descriptive and scalable.

Room Escape Adventure

'Hit ESC' is a click and point game created entirely on Powerpoint with no additional scripting and all images used are within Creative Commons.

SEPT 2019

As a programmer I made use of Powerpoint's multiple animations and buttons to create gameful interactions.

Summary

Prior to the Game Jam, some students have mentioned to me that although thwy would have liked to make games, they were neither coders nor artists. We took it as a challenge to create a game using Powerpoint, a tool many students were familiar with as an example that anyone can start making games. We explored as many game mechanics as possible that were translatable to a click and point interface and in the end we created Hit ESC which was a series of mini-games within a room escape adventure from dialogue systems and puzzles, to shooter games. Hit ESC's narrative asks the player to survive a regaular work day as a software developer by navigating minor inconveniences.

Team:

Tina (myself): Development

David Dufour: Art, Narrative

Gameplay

There are 5 mini-games in the series.
  1. Find items need to go to work in an object finding game.
  2. Navigate traffice using arrow keys to choose the free lanes in a driving simulation.
  3. Find items to remind you of your password to log into the computer.
  4. Close popups before the timer runs out to finish logging in, in a shooter game.
  5. Get out of an uncomfortable situation with a co-worker by choosing the correct dialogue options.
  6. Find items which would help you escape from a locked supply closet.
  7. Figure out which e-mail to forward to your coworker through a logic puzzle.

The player is instructed to hit ESC to excit the Powerpoint file.

Impact

Despite its simplicity, Hit ESC has made a large impact. Hit ESC has:

  • Been displayed for students and visitors at GI game jam showcases
  • Inspired non-coders to make games at game jams. New teams in 2020 Global Game Jam used Powerpoint as a tool.
  • Been used in computer science classrooms to demonstrate that Powerpoint is a fast prototyping tool.
  • Been recognized by Microsoft for its innovative use of Powerpoint:

Reflection

It was rewarding to later see teams of non-coders say they used Powerpoint as a game-making tool after seeing Hit ESC. We achieved our goal of showing that almost anyone can make games. I was also happy to hear university-level lecturers use the game file to teach their students about prototyping.

What I learned
I learned a lot about Powerpoint and its animation and 3D libraries . In the future, I would love to create interactive presentations for group meetings or online courses.

What I would have done differently
This project was almost designed in reverse, where we identified available mechanics before designing gameplay. With my current knowledge fo Powerpoint's features, I would have designed for a looped experience instead of linear minigames by making use of linked slides. Embedding other Microsoft product files like excel and word would also be worth exploring.
